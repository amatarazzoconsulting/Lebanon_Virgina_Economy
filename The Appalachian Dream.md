# Part One: The Virginia Appalachian Context

## Chapter 1: Introduction - Virginia's Mountains of Opportunity

The Appalachian Mountains of Virginia represent one of the most significant and resilient landscapes in the eastern United States, stretching across the western portion of the Commonwealth in a sweeping arc of ancient ridges, fertile valleys, and forested peaks that have shaped the history, culture, and economy of the region for centuries. This extraordinary landscape encompasses 25 counties and eight independent cities across Virginia, forming a vital corridor that connects the Commonwealth's diverse regions while maintaining a distinctive identity rooted in the mountains' geological history and the resilience of its people. The Virginia Appalachian region is home to communities that have weathered the boom and bust cycles of coal mining, timber extraction, and manufacturing, emerging with a determination to build a new economy that honors their heritage while embracing the opportunities of the 21st century.

The economic challenges facing Virginia's Appalachian counties are substantial, yet the region possesses unique advantages—strategic location, natural beauty, cultural heritage, and a skilled workforce—that position it for a renaissance built on diversification and innovation. The transition away from coal dependence has been painful, but it has also created opportunities for new industries, including advanced manufacturing, information technology, tourism, and sustainable agriculture. The Appalachian Dream is about harnessing these opportunities to create a future where the children of the mountains can find meaningful work and build prosperous lives without leaving their homeland.

## Chapter 2: The Virginia Appalachian Region - Geography, Geology, and Natural Assets

### 2.1 The Geological Foundation

The Virginia Appalachian region is characterized by three distinct geological provinces: the Appalachian Plateau, the Valley and Ridge Province, and the Blue Ridge Province, each with unique features that have shaped settlement patterns and economic activities. The Appalachian Plateau, located in the far western portion of Virginia, includes the Cumberland and Allegheny mountains and contains the coal deposits that have been the economic foundation of the region for over a century. The Valley and Ridge Province contains the Clinch Mountains, which have provided the setting for Lebanon and other communities, with fertile valleys that have supported agriculture since the earliest European settlement. The Blue Ridge Province, characterized by its dramatic peaks and scenic beauty, has become a major tourism destination, with the Blue Ridge Parkway drawing millions of visitors annually.

### 2.2 Forests, Wildlife, and Natural Resources

The Virginia Appalachian region contains some of the most biologically diverse forests in North America, supporting a rich array of wildlife that provides both economic value through tourism and ecological value through ecosystem services. The forests are home to species including black bears, wild turkeys, deer, and numerous songbirds, while the Clinch River is recognized as one of the most biodiverse river systems in North America. The region's natural beauty and biodiversity provide a foundation for tourism and outdoor recreation, which have become increasingly important economic sectors.

# Part Two: Culture, Heritage, and Living Traditions

## Chapter 3: The Indigenous Peoples of the Virginia Appalachians

### 3.1 The Cherokee and Yuchi Legacy

The Virginia Appalachian region has been home to Native American peoples for thousands of years, with the Cherokee and Yuchi nations being among the most prominent groups to inhabit the mountains and valleys of Southwest Virginia. The Cherokee, who called the mountains their ancestral homeland, created a sophisticated society with complex political structures, agricultural systems, and spiritual traditions that were intimately connected to the natural world. The Yuchi people, who also inhabited the region, contributed to the cultural mosaic of the mountains with their own distinct traditions, languages, and ways of life. The presence of these peoples shaped the region's identity long before European settlement.

### 3.2 Place Names and Cultural Imprint

The legacy of Native American presence in the Virginia Appalachians is preserved in the place names that dot the landscape, serving as a permanent reminder of the peoples who first called these mountains home. Names like Clinch Mountain, the Clinch River, and the Holston River reflect the linguistic heritage of the indigenous peoples, while the Cherokee name for the Clinch River, "Ollongee," provides a direct connection to the region's Native American past. The town of Lebanon itself, while named for the biblical cedars, sits in a landscape that was known and named by the Cherokee long before European settlement. Recognizing and preserving this heritage is an essential part of honoring the region's complete history.

### 3.3 Cultural Revitalization and Economic Potential

The revitalization of Native American cultural heritage in the Virginia Appalachians offers significant economic potential through cultural tourism and the preservation of traditional crafts. The Cherokee and Yuchi legacy can be celebrated through interpretive centers, cultural festivals, and educational programs that attract visitors while honoring the region's indigenous heritage. The preservation of traditional crafts, including pottery, weaving, and beadwork, provides economic opportunities for artisans while maintaining cultural traditions. The integration of Native American heritage into the region's tourism economy enriches the visitor experience while ensuring that the contributions of the region's first peoples are recognized and honored.

## Chapter 4: The Animal Economy - Horses, Ponies, and Livestock

### 4.1 The Horse Culture of the Virginia Mountains

The Virginia Appalachian region has a rich tradition of horse culture that has evolved from the working animals of early settlers to the recreational and competitive horses of today. The mountains provided the setting for the development of distinctive breeds, including the Virginia Highlander, which has been recognized as the "official horse" of Virginia in a bill introduced in the General Assembly. The bill, submitted by Delegate Tony Wilt in January 2026, encourages the breeding, showing, and appreciation of this native breed. The horse economy in the region includes breeding, training, showing, and trail riding, with significant economic impact through tourism, events, and the equine supply chain. The preservation and promotion of this horse culture is essential for maintaining the region's cultural identity and economic vitality.

### 4.2 Pony Riders and the Mountain Pony Tradition

The tradition of pony riders and mountain ponies has deep roots in the Appalachian region, with generations of families raising and training ponies for work, transportation, and recreation. The resilient mountain pony, bred for the rugged terrain, is a living link to the region's past and a vital part of its cultural identity. Pony riding events, festivals, and competitions provide opportunities for communities to come together and celebrate their heritage while generating economic activity through tourism. The preservation of the mountain pony tradition requires support for breeding programs, educational initiatives, and events that maintain and promote this distinctive heritage.

### 4.3 Cattle and Livestock Production

Livestock production is the largest sector of Virginia Appalachian agriculture, with cattle and calves representing the primary source of agricultural income for many counties. The Virginia Quality Assured feeder cattle program has marketed over 125,000 head since 1997, generating $6.4 million in value-added income. The cattle industry is a vital part of the region's economy and culture, with ranchers who represent generations of commitment to the land and the animals they raise. The expansion of premium beef production, automation, and value-added processing provides opportunities for the industry to continue growing while maintaining the traditions that define it.

## Chapter 5: The Cultural Economy - Music, Crafts, and Community

### 5.1 The Crooked Road and Musical Heritage

The Virginia Appalachian region is the birthplace of a musical tradition that has influenced the world, with the Crooked Road: Virginia's Heritage Music Trail serving as the foundation of the region's cultural economy. The 333-mile driving trail links over 60 music venues across Southwest Virginia, celebrating the region's unique musical and cultural heritage. The Crooked Road's affiliated venues and festivals program provides a network for promoting the region's musical traditions, supporting events that attract visitors and sustain local economies. The preservation and promotion of this musical heritage is essential for maintaining the region's cultural identity and economic vitality.

### 5.2 'Round the Mountain and Artisan Traditions

The Virginia Appalachian region has a rich tradition of artisan crafts, with the 'Round the Mountain Artisan Network providing a framework for promoting and supporting the region's artisans. The artisan economy includes pottery, woodworking, quilting, jewelry, and other crafts that have been practiced in the mountains for generations. The Southwest Virginia Cultural Center & Marketplace serves as a hub for the artisan network, showcasing the products of the region's craftspeople and providing a retail experience that appeals to visitors. The preservation and promotion of artisan traditions provides economic opportunities for craftspeople while maintaining the region's cultural identity.

### 5.3 Festivals, Events, and Community Gatherings

Festivals and events are a vital part of the region's cultural economy, with events such as Cedar Fest bringing visitors from across the region and supporting local businesses. The Virginia Tourism Corporation's Special Events and Festivals Sponsorship Program provides matching grant funds for events that stimulate overnight visitation, with Cedar Fest receiving a $5,000 grant requiring a $5,000 match. The success of these events demonstrates the economic potential of cultural tourism and the importance of community engagement in supporting the region's economy.

# Part Three: The Virginia Appalachian Economy - History, Resources, and Transformation

## Chapter 6: The Historical Evolution of Virginia's Mountain Economy

The economic development of Virginia's Appalachian region was profoundly shaped by the arrival of railroads and industrialization in the late nineteenth and early twentieth centuries, transforming isolated mountain communities into industrial centers serving the nation's growing energy and manufacturing needs. The Norfolk and Western Railroad, which extended into Southwest Virginia in the 1880s, opened the region's coal fields to national markets and created the transportation infrastructure that would define the region's economy for generations. The coal industry emerged as the dominant economic force, with the vast bituminous coal deposits of Southwest Virginia becoming a key resource for the nation's industrial growth.

The timber industry also played a significant role in the region's economy, with the forests of Southwest Virginia providing lumber for construction, paper production, and other uses. The combination of coal and timber extraction established extractive industries as the foundation of the region's economy, creating wealth for absentee owners while leaving local communities vulnerable to the boom-and-bust cycles that would characterize the region's economic history. The arrival of manufacturing in the mid-20th century provided some diversification, with industries including textiles, furniture, and electronics establishing operations in the region, but the underlying pattern of economic dependence on external markets persisted.

The decline of the coal industry in the late 20th and early 21st centuries created economic distress in communities that had depended on mining for generations. The closure of mines and the loss of mining jobs led to population decline, economic dislocation, and social challenges that threatened the viability of communities throughout the region. The economic transformation of Virginia's Appalachian region continues today, as communities seek to diversify their economies and build a sustainable future that honors their heritage while embracing new opportunities.

## Chapter 7: The Virginia Appalachian Regional Commission and Economic Classification

The Appalachian Regional Commission (ARC) provides a framework for understanding the economic challenges facing Virginia's Appalachian counties, using a classification system that identifies communities in greatest need of investment and support. In fiscal year 2026, the ARC's classification system placed Russell County in the "At-Risk" category with a composite index value of 156.0, ranking 2,677th among 3,106 U.S. counties. Buchanan County and Dickenson County were classified as "Distressed," ranking in the worst 10 percent of all U.S. counties on the composite index. The data reveals a region where per capita market income averages just 57.4 percent of the U.S. average, and poverty rates reach 17.0 percent, substantially higher than the national average of 12.4 percent.

The ARC's investment strategy focuses on five strategic goals: economic diversification, workforce development, infrastructure improvement, natural and cultural asset preservation, and community capacity building. Through programs such as the POWER Initiative, the ARC has funded projects that support economic transition and create sustainable economic opportunities in distressed communities. For Virginia's Appalachian counties, the ARC's programs provide critical resources for developing new industries, creating jobs, and building the infrastructure needed for sustainable growth.

## Chapter 8: Emerging Industries and Next-Generation Economic Development

The Virginia Appalachian region is experiencing a renaissance of innovation that is transforming its economy and creating new opportunities for the 21st century. The expansion of Tate's data center components manufacturing operation in Russell County, supported by a $4 million VCEDA loan, demonstrates the potential for technology manufacturing to create jobs and economic activity. The modular housing manufacturing facility in Lebanon, supported by a $3 million VCEDA loan, represents a new industry that addresses the region's workforce housing needs while creating employment opportunities in modern construction.

The region's advanced broadband infrastructure, anchored by the CPC OptiNet fiber-optic network, provides the connectivity necessary for technology companies to locate and thrive in the region. The e-Region brand, developed by VCEDA, positions Southwest Virginia as one of the East Coast's new centers for electronic information technology, energy, education, and emerging specialty manufacturing. The region's proximity to major metropolitan areas and its strategic location within a day's drive of major markets provide advantages for businesses seeking to serve the eastern United States.

The agricultural sector is also undergoing transformation, with the adoption of precision agriculture technologies, the expansion of premium beef production, and the development of value-added processing creating new opportunities for farmers and communities. The Virginia Quality Assured feeder cattle program, the expansion of the dairy sector, and the development of agritourism experiences are all part of the region's agricultural renaissance. The integration of agriculture with tourism, manufacturing, and technology creates a diversified economic base that is more resilient than the extractive industries that dominated the region's economy for generations.

# Part Four: The Environmental Challenge and Path to Restoration

## Chapter 9: Air Quality - The Invisible Threat

The Virginia Appalachian region faces significant air quality challenges from coal-fired power plants, industrial sources, and vehicle emissions, with the potential for increased pollution if environmental regulations are rolled back. The Virginia Department of Environmental Quality monitors air quality across the state, with counties in the Southwest Virginia region experiencing elevated levels of particulate matter, sulfur dioxide, and nitrogen oxides. The health impacts of air pollution are severe, with elevated rates of respiratory disease, cardiovascular disease, and other health conditions in communities near industrial sources. The transition to cleaner energy sources and the implementation of pollution control technologies are essential for protecting public health and maintaining the region's quality of life.

The Appalachian mixed mesophytic forests, which cover much of the region, play a critical role in maintaining air quality by filtering pollutants from the atmosphere. The preservation and restoration of these forests is essential for maintaining air quality, providing ecosystem services that benefit both the region and the broader eastern United States. The federal Clean Air Act and state environmental regulations provide the framework for protecting air quality, and the enforcement of these regulations is essential for ensuring that all communities have access to clean, healthy air.

---

## Chapter 10: Water Quality and Waste Management

The waters of the Virginia Appalachian region, once crystal clear, face significant threats from agricultural runoff, mining waste, and other forms of pollution that affect both human health and aquatic ecosystems. The Clinch River, recognized as one of the most biodiverse river systems in North America with more than 50 species of freshwater mussels and numerous fish species, has been affected by pollution from mining operations, agriculture, and other sources. The valley fills from mountaintop removal mining represent one of the most significant threats to water quality, with mining spoil burying waterways and discharging pollution that wreaks havoc on aquatic life.

The Virginia Department of Environmental Quality, the Virginia Department of Health, and other agencies are working to address water quality challenges through the Clean Water Act and state water quality regulations. The restoration of the Clinch River and other waterways requires a comprehensive approach that addresses both the legacy of pollution and the ongoing threats to water quality. The participation of local communities, conservation organizations, and government agencies is essential for achieving the water quality improvements needed to protect public health and the region's ecological treasures.
